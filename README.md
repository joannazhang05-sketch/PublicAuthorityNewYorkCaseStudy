# NYC Housing Authority Upward Mobility Differences (Case Study)

## Research Introduction
Separated by nothing more than Parsons Boulevard, two affordable housing developments in Fresh Meadows, Queens, New York, continue to operate today. Yet, despite sharing the same geographical location, being in walking distance to Queens College, and serving low-income families, they differ drastically in upward mobility. To the left of Parsons Boulevard, Pomonok Houses, created in 1952 by the New York City Housing Authority, has an upward mobility of children’s income into adulthood (at 35 years of age) of 34,000 USD. On the right side of the street, Electchester, created in the 1950s by Local 3 of the International Brotherhood of Electrical Workers, has an upward mobility of children’s income into adulthood (at 35 years of age) of 49,000 USD. As seen in Figure 1, using the Opportunity Atlas, though both housing developments were created in the 1950s and have the same geological location, children who grow up in Electchester earn roughly 14,000 USD more per year in household income at 35 years old in comparison to those raised in Pomonok.

Through the analysis of variables and topics studied throughout ECON 50, this research delves deeper into why there is a difference in output from these two affordable housing developments and the core aspects within each community. If both Pomonok Houses and Electchester Houses provide below-market housing, why do children from low-income families experience different adult outcomes?
